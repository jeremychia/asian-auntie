# Problem Statement

## The Core Problem

Users struggle to keep track of perishable items in their homes. Items expire without being used, leading to food waste and wasted money. When buying items from abroad or in bulk, it's especially hard to remember what's expiring and in what order.

### Pain Points

1. **Visibility**: No clear picture of what's in the apartment and when it expires
2. **Waste**: Items expire and get thrown away because the user forgot they had them
3. **Inefficiency**: Time spent wondering "what was that expiry date again?" when cooking
4. **Buying patterns**: Difficult to remember purchase history and identify patterns (e.g., "I always waste cilantro")
5. **Decision paralysis**: What should I cook tonight with what I have?

## Target User

### Primary User Persona

**Name**: Jeremy (You)

**Demographics**:
- Individual living situation (no household sharing concerns for MVP)
- Shops in bulk or from international sources
- Wants to minimize food waste
- Tech-savvy, willing to adopt new tools

**Pain Points**:
- Forgetful about what's in the fridge/pantry
- Buys items in bulk and loses track of quantity
- Wastes food regularly due to forgotten items
- Wants to be more intentional about cooking

**Goals**:
- Know what's about to expire before it's too late
- Make faster cooking decisions based on available ingredients
- Reduce food waste and money spent on expired items
- Understand personal buying/waste patterns over time

## Related Insights

- Users spend time in two contexts: **at home** (checking inventory, cooking) and **shopping** (remembering what to buy)
- Expiry dates come in multiple formats: "best by", "use by", "manufactured on" — interpretation varies
- Manual entry may be more realistic than barcode scanning for some items (international products)
- Photo capture has multiple potential uses: visual memory, verification, condition tracking
